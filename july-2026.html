Monthly Archive

July 2026

A continuing archive of words, meanings, and sentences gathered through July.

July 1

New Word: Peripatetic = (Adj.) Constantly moving rather than staying in one location.

New Sentence: My own life has been more peripatetic than theirs.

July 2

New Word: Precocity = (N.) exceptional intellectual, artistic, or emotional development in a child or young person.

New Sentence: The novel explores the burden of childhood precocity.

July 3

New Word: Limerick = (N.) a short, humorous poem of five lines with a distinctive rhyme and rhythm.

New Sentence: Perhaps as a result of reading Edward Lear, I became a fan of the limerick form.

July 6

New Word: Garret = (N.) a small room or apartment just below the roof of a house, typically in the attic.

New Sentence: It was the perfect author's garret.

July 7

New Word: Onanistic = (Adj.) relating to masturbation or self-gratification.

New Sentence: These self-glorifying prose fantasies offered a fleeting, onanistic comfort, usually followed by a pang of shame.

July 8

New Word: Hostelry = (N.) an inn, tavern, or hotel, especially an old-fashioned or traditional one.

New Sentence: And on that journey of fifteen-hour bus rides and humble hostelries, Midnight’s Children was born.

July 9

New Word: Garrulous = (Adj.) Excessively talkative

New Sentence: With that immodest proposal the novel’s tone of voice was comically assertive and unrelentingly garrulous.

July 10

New Word: Pathos = (N.) Somethig in a speech, film, music that stirs the emotion of sadness, pity and compassion. It is not sadness, but what evokes that emotion.

New Sentence: Dickens combined comedy with profound pathos.

July 13

New Word: Coat-tails = (N.) to ride (or hang on) someone's coattails... to benefit from another person's success, influence, or popularity rather than through your own efforts.

New Sentence: Only thing I could do was to let him rip, to let that omnivorous spirit loose on the page, and to hang on to his coat-tails as he ran.

July 14

New Word: Tirade = (N.) A long, angry and often critical speech or outburst.

New Sentence: We can see through Alex Portnoy’s tirade and understand that his creator sees his women with more profundity and passion than Alex does.

July 15

New Word: Profundity = (N.) great depth of thought, insight, wisdom, or meaning.

New Sentence: Despite its simplicity, the speech possessed unexpected profundity.

July 16

New Word: Faustian = (Adj.) a moral compromise to gain something worldly. Based on the character of German legend Faust who sold his soul to the devil in return of knowledge and power. A costly compromise.

New Sentence: Faustian bargain, Faustian quest. The film explores the Faustian pursuit of fame

July 17

New Word: Kakistocracy = (N.) Government by the worst, least qualified and most stupid people; opposite is meritocracy.

New Sentence: Commentators reached for the term kakistocracy to express their frustration with political leadership.

July 20

New Word: Mnemonic = (N. & Adj.) A memory aid—a word, phrase, pattern, rhyme, or technique that helps you remember information.

New Sentence: Medical students rely on mnemonics to remember complex anatomy.

July 21

New Word: Hallowed = (Adj.) Greatly revered, deeply respected, or considered sacred. Could be used instead of 'holy'

New Sentence: They walked onto the hallowed turf of Wembley.

July 22

New Word: red herring = (N.) something that distracts attention from the real issue or deliberately leads people in the wrong direction.

New Sentence: The detective realised the missing necklace was a red herring.

June 23

New Word: xxxxxx = (V.) to encroach upon or infringe upon someone else's rights, freedom, time, or space.

New Sentence: Public events impinge so directly on our private lives.

June 24

New Word: Demagogue = (N.) a political leader who gains power by appealing to people's emotions, fears, or prejudices rather than by using reasoned arguments or sound policies.

New Sentence: A celebrity president who is a populist demagogue.

June 25

New Word: massif = (N.) A principal mountain mass bounded by fault lines which move as one unit.

New Sentence: The details are doing more work here than the headline.

June 26

New Word: Disingenuous = (Adj.) Lacking in frankness, candour; insincere

New Sentence: Don't bury the lead.

June 29

New Word: Balmy = (Adj.) Pleasantly warm and mild.

New Sentence: Reading should be subject not to our plans but to our whims.

June 30

New Word: Irreverently = (Adv.) In a cheeky and plafully disrespectful way, not in a hostile way.

New Sentence: Some one who comes from outside has no idea where the bodies are buried.
